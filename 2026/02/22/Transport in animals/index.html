Topic 8 – Transport in Mammals

The Circulatory System

Closed Double Circulatory System

  • Mammals have a closed double circulatory system

  • The system consists of:

    • heart
    • blood
    • blood vessels (arteries, arterioles, capillaries, venules, veins)

Closed circulatory system

  • Blood is always contained within blood vessels
  • Allows higher pressure and faster transport

Double circulation

  • Blood passes through the heart twice in one complete circulation:

    1. Pulmonary circulation
    2. Systemic circulation

**Advantages **

  • High systemic blood pressure
  • Faster transport of oxygen and nutrients
  • Supports a high metabolic rate

Pulmonary and Systemic Circulation

Pulmonary circulation

  • Right ventricle → lungs → left atrium
  • Low pressure
  • Prevents damage to lung capillaries

Systemic circulation

  • Left ventricle → body tissues → right atrium
  • High pressure
  • Efficient delivery of oxygen and nutrients

Main Blood Vessels

Vessel Function
Pulmonary artery Carries deoxygenated blood from heart to lungs
Pulmonary vein Carries oxygenated blood from lungs to heart
Aorta Carries oxygenated blood from heart to body
Vena cava Carries deoxygenated blood from body to heart

Recognition of Blood Vessels

Artery

  • Thick wall
  • Thick tunica media
  • Narrow lumen
  • Circular shape
  • No valves

Vein

  • Thin wall
  • Large lumen
  • Irregular shape
  • Valves present

Capillary

  • Wall one cell thick (endothelium only)
  • Very narrow lumen
  • Red blood cells pass in single file

Structure Related to Function

Elastic arteries

  • Many elastic fibres
  • Stretch during systole
  • Recoil during diastole
  • Maintain continuous blood flow and pressure

Muscular arteries

  • Thick smooth muscle layer
  • Vasoconstriction and vasodilation
  • Control blood flow to organs

Arterioles

  • Very muscular walls
  • Regulate blood distribution

Veins

  • Thin walls
  • Large lumen
  • Valves prevent backflow
  • Skeletal muscle contraction aids blood return

Capillaries

  • One cell thick → short diffusion distance
  • Permeable walls
  • Narrow lumen → slow blood flow
  • Large total surface area

Blood Cells

Red Blood Cells

  • Diameter ≈ 7 µm
  • Biconcave disc shape → increased surface area
  • No nucleus → more space for haemoglobin
  • No mitochondria → do not use oxygen transported
  • Flexible → pass through capillaries

Neutrophils

  • Lobed nucleus
  • Carry out phagocytosis

Lymphocytes

  • Large nucleus
  • Produce antibodies

Monocytes

  • Kidney-shaped nucleus
  • Differentiate into macrophages

Water in Transport

Properties of water

  • Good solvent for polar substances
  • High specific heat capacity

Importance

  • Transport of glucose, amino acids, ions
  • Maintains stable body temperature

Tissue Fluid

Functions

  • Medium for exchange between blood and cells
  • Supplies oxygen and nutrients
  • Removes carbon dioxide and waste products

Formation of Tissue Fluid

  1. High hydrostatic pressure at arteriole end of capillary
  2. Plasma forced out of capillary walls
  3. Large proteins remain in capillaries
  4. Fluid surrounds cells → tissue fluid
  5. Lower pressure at venule end allows reabsorption
  6. Oncotic pressure draws fluid back into capillaries

Transport of Oxygen

Haemoglobin

  • Conjugated protein
  • Quaternary structure
  • Four haem groups
  • Each haem group binds one oxygen molecule

Oxygen Dissociation Curve

  • S-shaped (sigmoid) curve

  • Shows relationship between oxygen saturation and partial pressure of oxygen

  • Cooperative binding:

    • Binding of first oxygen increases affinity for next oxygen

In lungs

  • High pO₂
  • Haemoglobin becomes saturated

In respiring tissues

  • Low pO₂
  • Oxygen is released

Importance

  • Steep section allows large oxygen unloading with small drop in pO₂

Bohr Shift

  • Increased CO₂ concentration
  • CO₂ lowers pH
  • Oxygen dissociation curve shifts to the right
  • Haemoglobin has lower affinity for oxygen

Importance

  • More oxygen released to actively respiring tissues

Transport of Carbon Dioxide

Methods of CO₂ Transport

Method Approx. %
As hydrogencarbonate ions ~70%
As carbaminohaemoglobin ~23%
Dissolved in plasma ~7%

Role of Carbonic Anhydrase

  • Catalyses:

Haemoglobinic Acid

  • Hydrogen ions bind to haemoglobin

Carbaminohaemoglobin

  • Carbon dioxide binds to globin chains of haemoglobin

Chloride Shift

  • Hydrogencarbonate ions diffuse out of red blood cells
  • Chloride ions diffuse into red blood cells
  • Maintains electrical neutrality

Importance

  • Allows continued transport of CO₂ in blood

Structure of the Heart

External Structure

  • Pericardium
  • Coronary arteries supply heart muscle

Internal Structure

  • Four chambers:

    • Left atrium
    • Right atrium
    • Left ventricle
    • Right ventricle

  • Septum separates left and right sides

  • Valves:

    • Atrioventricular (AV) valves
    • Semilunar valves

Wall Thickness

  • Atria: thin walls → receive blood

  • Ventricles: thick walls → pump blood

  • Left ventricle:

    • Thickest wall
    • Pumps blood at high pressure to whole body

Cardiac Cycle

Definitions

  • Systole: contraction
  • Diastole: relaxation

Stages

  1. Atrial systole
  2. Ventricular systole
  3. Diastole

Valves

  • AV valves close → “lub”
  • Semilunar valves close → “dub”

Control of Heartbeat

  • Sinoatrial node (SAN)

    • Acts as pacemaker
    • Initiates heartbeat

  • Atrioventricular node (AVN)

    • Delays impulse
    • Allows ventricles to fill

  • Purkyne tissue

    • Conducts impulse rapidly
    • Causes coordinated ventricular contraction
