Circulatory system/cardiovascular system

Posted on 2025-05-06 Edited on 2025-05-13 In IGCSE , Biology

Revision note for Edexcel IGCSE Biology.

discussing circulatory system, including blood, vessels and heart.

Blood

plasma

  • pale yellow liquid
  • carrying everything that needs transporting around hunan body:
    • blood cells:
      • red blood cell
      • white blood cell
      • platelets
      • digested food products - from gut to all body cells
      • carbon dioxide
      • urea - from liver to the kidneys (be removed in the urine)
      • hormones
      • heat energy

platelets

  • small fragments of cells
  • help blood clotting
    • stop body losing too much blood
    • prevent microorganisms from entering the wound
    • In a clot:
      • platelets are held together by a protein called fibrin
      • other proteins call clotting factors are needed to work properly in this process

red blood cell

  • carry and transport oxygen from the lung to all the cells in the body
  • adaptational features:
    • small with a biconcave shape
      • larger surface area for absorbing and releasing oxygen.
    • contain haemoglobin
      • contain iron - gives blood its colour
      • reacts with oxygen to become oxyhaemoglobin
      • reverse reaction happens to release oxygen to the cells
    • no nucleus
      • more space for more haemoglobin
      • carry more oxygen

White blood cells

  • most important part of immune system
  • phagocytes
    • ingest pathogens
  • lymphocytes
    • produce antibodies

more white blood cells’ information in immune system part.

Vessels

Arteries branch into capillaries, and capillaries eventually join upp to form veins

Arteries

  • features:
    • carry the blood away from the heart
    • carry oxygenated blood (except the pulmonary artery)
    • transport blood in high pressure
    • transport blood in fast speed
    • thick muscular walls
      • contain elastic fibres
    • narrow lumen
  • adaptational features:
    • thick muscular walls containing elastic fibres
      • withstand high pressure of blood
      • maintain high pressure of blood
    • narrow lumen
      • maintain high pressure
  • aorta - largest artery in the body

Veins

  • features:
    • carry blood toward the heat
    • carry deoxygenated blood (except pulmonary vein)
    • transport blood in low pressure
    • transport blood in slow speed
    • thin walls
    • large lumen
    • contain valves
  • adaptational features:
    • large lumen:
      • reduce resistance to blood flow under low pressure
    • valves:
      • prevent the backflow of blood
  • vena cava - largest vein in the body

Capillaries

  • features:
    • very tiny
    • carry both oxygenated and deoxygenated blood
    • transport blood in low pressure
    • transport blood in slow speed
    • thin walls
      • one cell thick
    • carry blood very close to every cell
      • exchange substances with cells
    • supply food and oxygen
    • take away wastes (e.g. CO_2)
  • adaptational features:
    • one-cell-thick walls
      • decrease diffusion distance
      • increase rate of diffusion
    • permeable walls
      • substances are easy to diffuse in and out

Heart

cardiac: heart

structure

left atrium

  • connected with pulmonary vein
  • receive oxygenated blood from lungs

left ventricle

  • connected to aorta
  • pumped out oxygenated blood around the body
  • adaptational features:
    • thick wall - more muscles
      • higher pressure
      • pump blood around the whole body - long distance

right atrium

  • connected to vana cava
  • receive deoxygenated blood from the body

right ventricle

  • connected to pulmonary artery
  • pumped out deoxygenated blood to the lungs

Valves

  • preventing blood flowing backwards
  • in main vessels:
    • semi-lunar valve
      • pulmonary artery
      • aorta
  • inside heart:
    • bicuspid valve == left atrium ⟶\longrightarrow⟶ left ventricle
    • tricuspid valve == right atrium ⟶\longrightarrow⟶ right ventricle

coronary heart disease (CHD)

  • coronary arteries:
    • blood vessels supplying blood(oxygen,glucose) to the heart muscle (cardiac muscle)
  • coronary arteries get blocked by layers of fatty material building up
    • formed from cholesterol
    • two source of cholesterol
      • dietary cholesterol (from eaten animal products)
      • cholesterol synthesised by the liver
  • angina
    • partial blockage
    • restricted blood flow to the cardiac muscle cells
    • severe chest pains
  • heart attack
    • complete blockage
    • lack of oxygen
    • heart cells can not respire aerobically

risk factors:

  • diet high in saturated fat
    • fatty deposits inside arteries
  • smoking
    • increases blood pressure
    • cause damage to the inside of the coronary arteries
    • fatty deposits inside arteries
    • carbon monoxide will decrease the oxygen capacity of red blood cells

circulatory system

lesser circuit (circuit through lung)

  • deoxygenated blood flow through vena cava into the right atrium
  • right atrium contracts and blood flows through tricuspid valve into right ventricle
  • right ventricle contracts and blood flows through the semilunar valve in the pulmonary artery
  • blood flows through the pulmonary artery into the lungs, through the capillaries around alveoli
  • gas exchange - deoxygenated blood ⟶\longrightarrow⟶ oxygenated blood
  • oxygenated blood flows back through teh pulmonary vein into the left atrium

vena cava ⟶\longrightarrow⟶ right atrium ⟶\longrightarrow⟶ right ventricle ⟶\longrightarrow⟶ pulmonary artery ⟶\longrightarrow⟶ lungs ⟶\longrightarrow⟶ pulmonary vein ⟶\longrightarrow⟶ left atrium ⟶\longrightarrow⟶ left ventricle
valves: tricuspid valve / semilunar valve

greater circuit (circuit through the whole body)

  • left atrium ⟶\longrightarrow⟶ left ventricle through bicuspid valve
  • left ventricle ⟶\longrightarrow⟶ aorta, through the semi-lunar valve
  • aorta ⟶\longrightarrow⟶ rest of body
    • aorta ⟶\longrightarrow⟶ brain ⟶\longrightarrow⟶ brain vein ⟶\longrightarrow⟶ vena cava
    • aorta ⟶\longrightarrow⟶ hepatic artery ⟶\longrightarrow⟶ liver ⟶\longrightarrow⟶ hepatic vein ⟶\longrightarrow⟶ vena cava
    • aorta ⟶\longrightarrow⟶ mesenteric artery ⟶\longrightarrow⟶ gut ⟶\longrightarrow⟶ hepatic portal vein ⟶\longrightarrow⟶ liver ⟶\longrightarrow⟶ hepatic vein ⟶\longrightarrow⟶ vena cava
    • aorta ⟶\longrightarrow⟶ renal artery ⟶\longrightarrow⟶ kidney ⟶\longrightarrow⟶ renal vein ⟶\longrightarrow⟶ vena cava
Organ vessels in vessels out
Heart Vena cava pulmonary vein
Lung pulmonary artery pulmonary vein
kidney renal artery renal vain
gut mesenteric artery hepatic portal vein

heart rate

Heart rate :
measured by counting the number of times a heart beats in a minute (bpm)

Pacemaker:

  • a group of cells in teh right atrium
  • coordinate the contraction of heart muscle
    • by sending electrical impulses which initiate a contraction in the cardiac muscle
  • control the natural resting heart rate

Heart when exercising

  • heart pumps blood to cells:
    • supply oxygen and glucose
    • remove respiratory waste
  • factors:
    • exercise
    • adrenaline level
  • during exercise:
    • heart rate increase ⟶\longrightarrow⟶ more frequent
    • volume of blood increase ⟶\longrightarrow⟶ larger volume
  • after exercise:
    • heart rate remain high for a period of time
      • to break down lactic acid from anaerobic respiration (oxygen required)

moderate exercise -mainly aerobic respiration
intense exercise -mainly anaerobic respiration
